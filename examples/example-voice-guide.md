# Voice Guide -- benchcraftworkshop.com


## Tone register

Direct and authoritative. States opinions as measured conclusions, not hedged
possibilities. Conversational but not casual -- assumes the reader is a
competent adult who wants to learn, not a child who needs encouragement.
Occasional dry humor, never forced. Warm but not effusive.


## Sentence patterns

Average sentence length: 14 words. Mix of short declarative sentences
(8--10 words) for emphasis and medium compound sentences (18--22 words) for
explanation. Rarely exceeds 25 words. When a sentence gets long, it earns its
length with specific detail, not filler.

Rhythm: short-short-medium is the characteristic pattern. A claim, a
reinforcement, then the evidence or elaboration.

Example: "The No. 4 is the workhorse. Every shop needs one. At 9-3/4 inches
long with a 2-inch blade, it handles everything from rough dimensioning to
final smoothing on boards under 18 inches."


## Paragraph patterns

2--4 sentences per paragraph. Leads with the claim; supporting detail follows.
Single-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis on key recommendations, not as a
default rhythm. No paragraph exceeds 5 sentences.

Intros are 2--3 short paragraphs that answer the query immediately, then
establish the page's scope. No throat-clearing.


## POV and person

First person throughout. "I" not "Sarah Chen." Avoids "we" except when
referring to a specific group ("we tested six planes over three months").

Addresses the reader as "you" when giving direct instruction: "Set the blade
to take a shaving 0.002 inches thick." Does not use "you" for empathy
padding ("You might be wondering...").


## Authority signals

Professional experience referenced when it adds information unavailable
elsewhere. Specific: names the project, the wood species, the measurement,
the outcome. "I've flattened 200+ workbench tops" is acceptable because the
number matters. "In my 20 years of experience" is forbidden -- it's
credentialism, not information.

Workshop teaching experience surfaces naturally: "Students in my weekend
dovetail class consistently struggle with..." -- this adds practitioner
data, not a credential.

Book references used sparingly and only when the book covers the topic in
more depth: "I cover the full sharpening progression in Chapter 4 of
The Unplugged Workshop." Never used as a sales pitch within educational
content.


## Evidence patterns

Names specific tools by manufacturer and model number: "Lie-Nielsen No. 4
smoothing plane" not "a quality smoothing plane." Cites measurements in
fractions and decimals as appropriate to the context (fractions for
woodworking dimensions, decimals for blade settings).

References specific wood species by name. Compares by observable properties:
"Cherry planes more smoothly than red oak because of its finer grain
structure" not "some woods are easier to work than others."

Does not cite external articles or studies. Authority comes from personal
testing and workshop observation, not from linking to other content.


## What NOT to do

- Never use superlatives: "the best", "absolutely essential", "must-have",
  "you need this"
- Never hedge with "might", "perhaps", "arguably", "it seems" -- state the
  opinion or omit it
- Never open with a question the reader didn't ask ("Have you ever struggled
  with sharpening?")
- Never use "game-changer", "life-changing", "revolutionary"
- Never address the reader with false enthusiasm ("Great news!")
- Never use "in this post", "this guide covers", or any meta-description
  of the content
- Never explain why the topic matters before addressing it. The reader is
  already here; they know why it matters.
- Never use exclamation points in body copy. Period.
