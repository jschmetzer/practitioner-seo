- "must-have"
- "you won't believe"
- "in this post"
- "in this article"
- "in this guide"
- "this guide covers"
- "this post will"
- "without further ado"
- "let's dive in"
- "let's get started"
- "at the end of the day"
- "it goes without saying"
- "in today's world"
- "in this day and age"
- "when it comes to"
- "it's no secret that"
- "the fact of the matter is"
- "needless to say"

- "groundbreaking"


## Forbidden constructions

- Rhetorical questions as openers (unless the question is the reader's actual
  query and the answer immediately follows)
- "Whether you're a beginner or a master craftsman" or any variation of the
  skill-level-spanning construction
- "Have you ever wondered" or similar false-curiosity openers
- "As we all know" or "Everyone knows that"
- "In my 20 years of experience" or any credential-as-opener construction.
  Cite the specific experience, not the duration.
- Explaining why the topic matters before addressing it. The reader is already
  here.
- Exclamation points in body copy. Period.
- "You might be wondering" or "You may be asking yourself" -- empathy padding


## Forbidden words (context-dependent)

- "literally" (when used as emphasis)
- "incredibly" / "amazingly" / "absolutely" (empty intensifiers)
- "essential" (when meaning "recommended" -- reserve for genuinely essential
  items, like a bench vise for hand tool work)
- "perfect" (nothing is perfect; say what makes it good)
- "simple" / "simply" (if it were simple, the reader wouldn't need the guide)
- "just" (minimizing word -- "just apply pressure" dismisses the skill
  involved)
- "obviously" / "of course" (if it's obvious, don't say it)
- "interestingly" (let the reader decide if it's interesting)
- "arguably" (either argue it or don't)
- "might" / "perhaps" / "it seems" (hedging -- state the opinion or omit it)


## Site-specific additions

- "woodworking journey" -- not a journey, it's a practice
- "passion project" -- Sarah builds furniture professionally; this isn't a
  hobby framing
- "DIY" in reference to professional techniques -- this site teaches craft,
  not weekend projects
- "content" when referring to what Sarah writes -- she writes guides, reviews,
  and articles, not "content"
